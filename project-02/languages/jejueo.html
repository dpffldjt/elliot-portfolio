Jejueo

History

Through establishment as Tamna, an independent country, in the first century AD, to alliances with kingdoms on the peninsula, subjugation to the Mongols, and finally annexation in the 14th century, Jeju island has had a long history of cultural development apart from the mainland of Korea. Shortly after that annexation, in the Joseon era, Korean speakers are recorded saying the language of Jeju is largely unintelligible to them.

This distinction between languages was only strengthened by the Joseon era policy of isolation of the island. For almost 200 years, up to 1825, the residents of the island were isolated, even restricted from leaving. Isolation like this leaves a people to develop their language without outside influence, and it was fertile soil for further linguistic divergence from the language being used on the peninsula.¹

The State of Jejueo

In the modern era, the official policy of South Korea is that Jejueo is a regional dialect of standard Korean. There is little acknowledgement of it as its own language and only minor governmental support for preserving it.²

While there is still hope for revitalisation, this hope dwindles each day, as we lose more and more of the remaining native speakers. Without greater research, and especially institutional support, this language that holds so much cultural and academic value may simply be lost to time.
