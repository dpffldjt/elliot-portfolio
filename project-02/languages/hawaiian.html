Hawaiian

A Brief History of the Hawaiian Language

In the Kingdom of Hawai‘i, literacy was greatly emphasised, and the literacy rate was as much as 90% in the late 19th century. When the US government overthrew the Hawaiian government, the institutions that facilitated these high literacy rates was dismantled. Less than 100 years later, only 32 children spoke Hawaiian.

With great effort and community support, the language then underwent a massive revitalisation effort, one of the most successful in the world. Grandparents who still spoke the language worked to teach the children, and when those children grew up, they taught their children, and today there are immersion schools across the islands and the University offers degrees taught fully in Hawaiian.¹

Revitalisation Makes Waves

While the University of Hawai‘i has a strong presence in the Hawaiian revitalisation space, it does not limit itself to only Hawaiian. While I was a student there, I took a class specifically on language revitalisation, and I heard about all the varied Pacific languages that graduate students were working to document, preserve, and sustain for future generations. One particular language interest of my professors, and incidentally my own, was Jejueo.

While not a Pacific language, the University has a well established Korean program. Jejueo, as the closest relative to Korean and an underdocumented endangered language, fit naturally into the research goals of the university. The current circumstances of Jejueo look dire, but the success of the Hawaiian revitalisation program proves that languages like Jejueo can come back from dire circumstances and thrive.
